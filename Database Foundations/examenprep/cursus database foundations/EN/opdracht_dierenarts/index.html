Group work modelling

Assignment

Within this course, you learned what a database is, how to model it, implement it, and how to write queries to add and extract data in and out of the database. In this project you will go through the entire process from data model to queries for a specific case. The case is described below, it is up to you to develop the requested database according to the learned process.

This project will be realized by groups of maximum 4 students. The groups will be determined by the lecturers. The deadline for submission of your result is on December 12, 2025 at 11:59 p.m.. You need to submit the result via the assignment ‘Project Assignment’ which you can find on Toledo. This project counts for 5 points. A project that is not submitted by the deadline will result in a 0. Retakes are not possible.

For the second exam chance, the points on the group assignment do not carry over and the exam consequently counts for 18 points. After all, there are two points on the portfolio and those are retained. For more info: see ECTS sheet (link on Toledo or via your ISP) and Toledo.

To be handed in

You need to work out the following things:

  1. A conceptual data model in which you develop the case below. The conceptual data model needs to be drawn in draw.io and submitted in pdf format with the name ‘DF_conceptual_team_XX.pdf’ where you replace XX with your team number.
  2. A logical data model that contains the translation from your conceptual data model to a relational database model. The logical data model needs to be drawn in draw.io and submitted in pdf format with the name ‘DF_logical_team_XX.pdf’.
  3. A physical data model that contains the translation from your logical data model to a database in PostgreSQL. You need to create the physical data model preferably in DBSchema and submit the resulting script with the name ‘DF_create_database_team_XX.sql’. Also provide a .png of your schema (in DBSchema: Model > Export as ... > PNG image) with the name ‘DF_physical_team_XX.png’.
  4. A second script that adds data to the created tables. This script, named ‘DF_insert_data_team_XX.sql’, populates the database.
  5. A third script ‘DF_delete_database_team_XX.sql’, that clears the created schema. It deletes the schema. You may not use a drop cascade, but must delete all the tables separately in the correct order.
  6. Five queries that extract a result from the database (see below). You collect the different queries in one script ‘DF_query_team_XX.sql’.
  7. A video of maximum 15 minutes named ‘DF_video_team_XX’ In which you give a demo of your final results (described above). The format of the video must be playable in VLC. In this video, first of all, you explain your models and your reasoning behind them. From the physical data model onwards you build the whole database through pgAdmin by using your scripts (table creation and data). Then you show the execution of your queries and finally you delete the schema with your delete script. Test your video recording setup well in advance (software, video format, microphone, ...)!

Bundle all of the files above into one ‘DF_project_team_XX.zip’ file. Submit this .zip file via Toledo (Assignments > Project (group)). One submission per group is sufficient.

Veterinary Practice

The case below describes a company, people, objects and processes. We intentionally left out a lot of details. What information does one want to track? Make meaningful choices yourself and motivate them.

The ‘Connect’ Veterinary Practice in Haasrode is a primary care practice. This means that they do not perform complex operations, but you can go to them for everything else:

  • illness
  • vaccination
  • annual check-up
  • sterilisation and castration
  • dental care
  • medication
  • sale of animal feed and advice
  • hospitalisation
  • imaging
  • laboratory tests
  • animal care
  • referral to a secondary care practice
  • …

There are currently seven veterinarians working at the practice, each with their own specialisation in addition to general basic care. There are also two animal carers and one employee who is responsible for administration, accounting and running the practice day-to-day. The usual details are kept for each staff member (name, address, telephone number, etc.). For the veterinarians, their specialisation is also recorded (animal species, laboratory or imaging techniques, etc.).

The practice also offers a limited range of animal feed and products, mainly specialised (and therefore somewhat more expensive) items.

People can only visit with their pets by appointment. This is possible during the practice's opening hours (9 a.m. to 12 p.m. and 1 p.m. to 6 p.m.), but a vet is always available outside these hours. At weekends, you can only take your animal to an on-call vet. A list of telephone numbers indicates who is available when.

Below, we tell a story with a lot of data (names, dates, products, etc.) to give you an idea of what is involved in running a veterinary practice. After all, you need to design a database that supports the entire operation of this practice. All the data from the story below must also be included in your final database . Of course, you will need to generate a lot of data yourself to fill the database with enough realistic data so that the queries give meaningful results. Where data is missing (name, time, date, product, etc.), you can fill it in yourself with your own creativity.

We, the lecturers of the OPO Database Foundations, are not veterinarians, so it is almost certain that the story below contains things that are medically inaccurate.

Today is 3 September 2025, 9 a.m. The Desmedt family (Kerkstraat 23, 3053 Haasrode, tel. 0455123456, wim.desmedt@gmail.com) has an appointment for their cat Maurice. Maurice is a Bengal (cat breed) who was born around January 2022 . This cat came in for a consultation on 28 August at 3 p.m. with blood in his urine. In addition, it would be a good idea to switch to special food, such as "Hill's Prescription Diet c/d Multicare", which greatly reduces the risk of bladder stones forming. Karen thinks this is an excellent suggestion and buys a 12 kg bag (price €80.29). Together with the cost of the check-up (€40), she pays a total of €120.29.

Data Generation

You need to fill all tables with sufficient data. That data should look realistic. Usually, a combination of manual work (typing in data yourself) and automatic data generation (via DBSchema's data generator or online tools like Mockaroo) delivers the best results. What is "sufficient", by the way? Hard to put a specific number on that ("You must have at least ... rows in that table"). We would say: When querying (see next point), you should be able to get meaningful results. For example, the tennis player database is really not large enough (only 14 players, 2 teams, 8 fines). The cooking club, on the other hand, is sufficiently large (173 partially hand-coded ingredients, 500 automatically generated members, 10 manually entered dishes, 1178 communities found via a CSV file on the site of bpost, ...)

Queries

Compile 5 sufficiently complex and meaningful queries to query your database. Try to use all SQL material: SELECT, WHERE, inner and outer JOINS, GROUP BY, HAVING, ORDER BY, limit number of rows, etc.

We emphasized two words here:

  1. complex: we really do expect queries that employ the whole arsenal of what we saw in the SQL classes and not just a statement where you select something from a table.
  2. meaningful: justify why for the veterinary practice the result of this query is important. Introduce each query with some explanation, e.g. ‘For the veterinary practice it is important to get an overview per ... because ...’.
